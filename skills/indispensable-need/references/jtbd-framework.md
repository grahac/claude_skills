# Jobs to Be Done Framework for Indispensable Need Analysis

## The Core Framework

A "job to be done" is the progress a person is trying to make in a particular circumstance. Jobs are not about the product — they're about the person's life.

### The Three Dimensions

**Functional**: What am I trying to accomplish?
- Task completion, efficiency, reliability
- "I need to send invoices to 50 clients monthly without errors"
- Measurable, observable, often the easiest to identify

**Emotional**: How do I want to feel?
- Personal feelings during and after the experience
- "I want to feel confident that nothing fell through the cracks"
- Often the real driver behind switching, even when people cite functional reasons

**Social**: How do I want to be perceived?
- How others see me, my work, my choices
- "I want my team to see me as organized and on top of things"
- Often invisible to the user themselves but powerfully shapes behavior

### The Indispensability Matrix

```
                    Low Importance          High Importance
                    ──────────────          ───────────────
High               Niche curiosity         INDISPENSABLE
Substitution       (people try it           NEED
Difficulty         but don't need it)       (can't quit, can't replace)

Low                Ignorable               Commodity
Substitution       (doesn't matter,        (matters but anything
Difficulty         easy to replace)         works — compete on price)
```

## How to Assess Importance

Signs a job is **high importance**:
- Failure has real consequences (lost money, missed deadlines, damaged relationships)
- People currently spend significant time/money/effort on it
- Users have strong emotional reactions when it goes wrong
- It's tied to their identity or livelihood
- Frequency is high (daily/weekly, not yearly)

Signs a job is **low importance**:
- People forget to do it and nothing bad happens
- The workaround is "just don't bother"
- No emotional charge around it
- Nice to have, would not notice if gone

## How to Assess Substitutability

Signs substitution is **difficult**:
- No competitor handles the specific edge case or constraint
- Alternatives require combining 3+ tools to match capability
- The learning curve or setup cost of switching is weeks, not hours
- Data or workflow is deeply embedded and non-portable
- The product's approach is architecturally different (not just a feature gap)

Signs substitution is **easy**:
- Multiple competitors offer nearly identical functionality
- A spreadsheet or manual process works "good enough"
- Switching requires minimal effort (export/import, same-day migration)
- The differentiators are cosmetic (UI polish, branding) not structural

## Interview Questions for Each Dimension

### Functional
- What were you trying to accomplish when you started using this?
- What were you using before? What made you switch?
- If this product disappeared tomorrow, what would you do instead?
- What's the most frustrating workaround you'd have to adopt?

### Emotional
- How did you feel before you had this solution?
- What's the worst thing that could happen if this fails?
- When does this product make you feel most relieved/confident/calm?
- What anxiety does it eliminate that alternatives don't?

### Social
- Has using this changed how your team/clients/peers perceive your work?
- Would you recommend this to someone you respect? Why?
- Is there social pressure to use this (or not use it)?
- Does this product make you look good in ways alternatives don't?

## Examples of Indispensable Needs

**Figma** (circa 2020):
- Functional: Real-time multiplayer design collaboration
- Emotional: Eliminated the anxiety of working on outdated files
- Social: Made designers look collaborative and modern vs. "still using Sketch"
- Indispensable need: The only tool where a designer and PM could be in the same file simultaneously. No substitute existed for real-time design collaboration.

**Notion** (early adopters):
- Functional: Single workspace for docs, databases, wikis, project management
- Emotional: Feeling of having "one place for everything" vs. tool sprawl anxiety
- Social: Teams that used Notion signaled they were organized and modern
- Indispensable need: For small teams, the only tool that replaced 4-5 separate tools without losing capability. The substitution cost was going back to fragmentation.

**Linear** (vs. Jira):
- Functional: Fast, keyboard-driven issue tracking
- Emotional: Relief from Jira's sluggishness and complexity — "it doesn't fight me"
- Social: Engineering teams using Linear signaled they cared about developer experience
- Indispensable need: Speed as a feature. No project management tool matched Linear's responsiveness, and for teams that valued flow state, that speed gap was intolerable to give up.

## Anti-Patterns in JTBD Analysis

- **Feature listing**: Describing what the product does instead of what job the user hires it for
- **Demographic targeting**: "Millennials who like productivity" is not a job
- **Solution-first thinking**: Starting from the product's capabilities instead of the user's circumstances
- **Ignoring non-consumption**: Sometimes the real competitor is "do nothing" or "use a spreadsheet"
- **Conflating frequency with importance**: A job done once a year (filing taxes) can be more important than a daily job (checking the weather)
